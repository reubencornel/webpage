1.2 Dijkstra's 8 queens method

The problem is to generate all configuration of the eight queen problem. Let the set of all configurations to be generated is Set A, we have to look for a set B with the following properties

  • Set A is a subset of Set B
  • Given an element of set B we should be able to identify it belongs to set A
  • We can make an operator that generates all the elements of set B

Things to note here are

  1. Set B must be larger than set A, but not too very big
  2. The decision criterion that decides if an element of Set B belongs to set A must be cheap
  3. The assumption here is that it is easier to generate elements of set B rather than generate elements of Set A. If it is difficult to generate elements of set B we would have to look at the problem from a higher level, such that we would have to generate elements of a set C which is the superset of Set B.

Coding skill is just one part of writing correct programs. The majority of the task involves

Every verification must have three steps

  • Maintainence
  • Termination

3 General Notes

3.1 Ten minute guide to managing your time

3.1.1 Chapter 1

  • Recognise the more the items that compete for your time the more time will speed by
  • Now is the only moment when you can take actions
  • Doing things faster economically is not the right way you could end up hurting your self for no reason
  • At every moment keep asking your self "What is the highest and best use of time right now?"
  • Trust your instincts
  • Any time management tool that is not up date is worthless

3.1.2 Chapter 2

  • Prioritizing is good but then the whole thing becomes a waste if there are too many priorities
  • Identify Priorities
    • Write down the entire list of things
    • Revisit the list and see whats important, if you feel that there is something that is not important then in all probability its not important
    • Restructure and redefine the list.Keep the language on the list postive
  • Establish a variety of goals to support your priorities
    • List out the goals
      • When listing out the goals make it a point to be very very specific.
  • Re-inforcing your goals
    • Be accountable to someone who is interested in listening to your goals
    • Visualize

3.1.3 Chapter 3

  • Things that compete for time fall into 4 categories
    • Urgent and important
    • Not urgent and important
    • Not important and urgent
    • Not urgent and not important
  • Pareto Principle: 80% of what a person achieves is because of the 20% of the time he spends on that
  • Systemize, systemize, systemize
    • Never manage what you can eliminate al together
  • The larger the organization the harder is it to be successful on your own
  • When joining an organization pick mentors, people who can help you out…and you add value to them

3.1.4 Chapter 4: Scheduling for results

  • If you don't have a timeline to your project it probably will never get done
  • This chapter was an over view of tools that can be used to perform scheduling.

3.1.5 Chapter 5: Getting organized

  • Everything in the universe is subject to entropy
  • Four things that could be done to every item
    • Act on it
    • Delegate it
    • File it
    • Recycle it
  • If something you're holding onto will truly help a person you're better of giving it away.

3.1.6 Chapter 7: Surviving information overload

  • There is more information being generated on earth every minute exceeds what you can absorb in the next 80 years
  • Set up filters so as to restrict what you see, and you're are not idundated with information
  • Even if you're are in a niche industry its difficult to keep up with all the information that is being generated.
    • Identify the top 20% of the information sources that would give you 80% of the information
    • Remove out stuff that you don't need

3.1.8 Chapter 9: Keep interruptions to a minimum

3.2 Accounting    ACCOUNTING

Notes from the GNUCash manual

  • There are five types of accounts
    • Assets: Things that you own
    • Liabilities: Things that I owe
    • Equity: Total net worth (= Assets - Liability)
    • Income
    • Expenses

Accounting Equation: Equity + (Income - Expenses) = Assets - Liability

  • The double entry method of accounting is based on this fact. That is when ever there is a change in an account, that change must get reflected into some other account. Also called the principle of balance.

3.3 Photography    PHOTOGRAPHY

  • Basic Rules
    • Simplify: A photograph conveys a message to the viewer, by simplifying an image we remove elements that might distract the viewer from the message of the subject.

      The most obvious way to do this is to get close.

    • Composition
      • Rule of thirds: Based on the golden mean.
      • The triangle: When a picture is composed as a rectangular frame, the triangle goes from one end of the rectangle to two opposite sides of the frame.
      • Frame with in a frame: Typically for landscapes, use existing elements to create a sort of frame around the subject.
      • Leading lines: Lines to guide the eyes through the photo.
      • Rythm: Use repeating form and shape to create interest in the photo
      • Negative space: Leave a lot of space around the object.
    • Can we do it better
      • Try out a different angle

8.3 Self Improvement

8.3.1 Zen Habits

8.3.1.1 Breathe    BREATHE

Breathing can transform your life. If you feel stressed out and overwhelmed, breathe. It will calm you and release the tensions. If you are worried about something coming up, or caught up in something that already happened, breathe. It will bring you back to the present. If you are discouraged and have forgotten your purpose in life, breathe. It will remind you about how precious life is, and that each breath in this life is a gift you need to appreciate. Make the most of this gift. If you have too many tasks to do, or are scattered during your workday, breathe. It will help bring you into focus, to concentrate on the most important task you need to be focusing on right now. If you are spending time with someone you love, breathe. It will allow you to be present with that person, rather than thinking about work or other things you need to do. If you are exercising, breathe. It will help you enjoy the exercise, and therefore stick with it for longer. If you are moving too fast, breathe. It will remind you to slow down, and enjoy life more. So breathe. And enjoy each moment of this life. They’re too fleeting and few to waste.

8.3.2 Advice about savings

  1. Establish a budget. I mean a serious, solid budget that every single one of your transactions is categorized into. This will help you identify areas that you are spending more than you want to in, and will further increase your rate of savings. Do this, and you establish a stronger basis for the rest of these steps. I suggest using Mint.com, as it does everything you need in terms of pulling in transactions, categorizing them and aggregating them into a budget.
  2. Find a good high-yield checking account with at least 4% APR (there are PLENTY out there), and use this as both savings and checking. This is where I put my emergency fund and any money that is waiting to be allocated. If you're intelligent and not impulsive, you don't need a clear separation between your savings and checking account.
  3. Establish a safety net. People suggest 6 months of current living expenses, although I'm comfortable with 3 months of current living expenses. I believe I can make that last long enough in an emergency. This money goes into the high-interest checking account, as you want it to be available at a moment's notice but still working for you.
  4. DO NOT SKIMP ON THE SAFETY NET. Don't jump directly into investing without having a safety net, because you will in effect be gambling with money you cannot afford to lose. Seriously, don't do this, because you could find yourself in a situation where you need to pay medical bills or repair your home after a disaster, and then you're stuck liquidating your investments, accepting whatever profits/losses they represent at the time, as well as incurring a significant taxable event. Don't do this.
  5. Once your emergency fund is complete, you need to sit down and make the hard choice of dividing up your current and future income among what I call Short Term savings, Long Term savings and Retirement Savings.
  6. Short term savings is just that, money that you want to be able to spend within the next 5-10 years. To figure this out, think hard on what big-ticket purchases you want to be able to make over the next 5-10 years, and write them down. After you figure out when and in what order you want them, you can figure out how much money you need to put into short term savings to be able to achieve them by those dates. If you would need to put away an unreasonable amount of money, you need to reconsider how soon you want them. Short Term savings goes into the high-yield checking account, along with the emergency fund. You do not invest the Short Term money, because you need it in the near term and can't afford for it to take a dip which causes you to have to wait for it to come back up.
  7. Retirement savings is how much money you want to put away for your retirement. This is simple but often painful to figure out. There are plenty of calculators online to help you figure out how much money you need to put away each paycheck to meet your retirement goals, based on how much money you want annually after retirement. If your employer offers any sort of matching contribution, do this as it is clearly free money. You can direct your retirement accounts yourself, as the investing operates on the same basic principles as your Long Term savings investing. Retirement savings goes into a retirement account, and you will not touch it until retirement unless it is a matter of life and death.
  8. Long Term savings is where I put everything that does not go into Short Term or Retirement. It is money that I am investing across a long-term timeline (10 years or longer), but which I want to be able to access before retirement. I suggest opening a brokerage account with Vanguard, as they have the best customer service I have ever encountered and many of their policies and attitudes towards Stocks, Funds and ETFs are visionary. Also they don't charge commission on investments made inside the Vanguard family of stocks/funds, which is great when you don't have a lot of money to invest. It would suck to incur a $7 fee each month when you're only putting $100 or $200 in, right?
  9. Don't follow the nonsense that Wall Street tells you about investing. I suggest reading The New Coffeehouse Investor for this. Picking hot stocks will not make the average person rich, it will bury you. In terms of directed mutual funds (groups of stocks that an expert directs, buying and selling individual stocks in an attempt to beat the market) very few actually beat the market, and most under-perform. The stock market is a very efficient creature. You will not beat it. Settle for taking the average return, which is still a better return than most expert stock pickers achieve. Read that book, it will teach you how to get a reliable and reasonable return without having to even look at your stock portfolio more than once or twice a year. I don't have time to spend every single day agonizing over stocks, do you?
